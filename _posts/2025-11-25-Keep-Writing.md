## Writing Essays on Geopolitics and a Canadian Law

I don't know if I ever wrote as much as I did during this week. The semester is coming to an end and so every lecture has their final assignments due in the next one or two weeks. Additionally I am working on my master's application, which i will talk about more in next week's blog. I thought it might be interesting to attach what I produced.

The first essay was for the course "The Real World of Politics: An Introduction". It is a first year course and I am overall not so sure I get that much out of it. It is very superficial covering a completely new topic every week: War, Democracy, Liberalism. Most new was this weeks discussion of Canada's Colonial past and how that is still a major topic today. Something I was not aware of at all when I came here. I first stumbled across it via a Land Acknowledgment. This is the one of the University of Toronto's:

"I (we) wish to acknowledge this land on which the University of Toronto operates. For thousands of years it has been the traditional land of the Huron-Wendat, the Seneca, and the Mississaugas of the Credit. Today, this meeting place is still the home to many Indigenous people from across Turtle Island and we are grateful to have the opportunity to work on this land.”

Over the course of the semester we had two in class assignments where we had to write an introduction to an essay and an argumentative paragraph. The final assignment was now to write a full essay. We could choose between three topics to write a roughly 900 word essay on. The one I chose had the following prompt:

"This month, the 30th annual United Nations Climate Change Conference (COP30) is taking place in Brazil. The COP meetings have succeeded in decreasing emissions in many industrialized states, but those reductions are not enough to keep global warming under 1.5C. This year, the promise of COP is undermined by the absence of many global leaders, including US President Donald Trump. If the second largest emitter of GHGs rejects climate science and decarbonization, other countries have less incentive to reduce their emissions. Drawing from class materials, the reference article, and external sources, explain why this logic is not necessarily true, and how decarbonization can advance, even without cooperation among states."

This was short in comparison to the 2500 word paper I was expected to write for "Energy Policy and Environment". The course gives a wide overview of how energy policy works, what has to be taken into consideration, and sets it into the context of what is currently going on in Canada. The lecturer is a policy analist at Greenpeace and super friendly. After every lecture, which end at 8 p.m., he gives the opportunity for interest people to stay and have a discussion on this week's topic. It is always super interesting to hear somebody who has been doing this for multiple decades, talk about his experiences and how he approaches making an impact. After two shorter essays, one on hope and the other on using a policy simulator, the final paper was to be on a new Canadian legislation: Bill C-5:

"For the major paper, you will assess how the federal government’s 2025 Building Canada Act (the second part of Bill C-5) will affect Canada’s energy transition. You should include consideration of:

- How does the legislation relate to the institution of federalism?
- How have various interests (e.g. provincial governments, businesses, environmentalists and Indigenous communities) responded to it and why have they taken these positions?
- What ideas have these interests mobilized to support their position?
- How will this legislation assist or impede the energy transition?

You will be expected to demonstrate an understanding of the concepts studied in the course and an ability to apply them to this topic, as well as an ability to identify and include other relevant research. Please note that you must correctly cite relevant material from the course’s required readings in this assignment. The essay should be no more than 2500 words."

<iframe src="{{ site.baseurl }}/assets/pdfs/FINAL_Major-Paper_Arved-Luetzen.pdf" width="100%" height="800px" frameborder="0"></iframe>

After spending over thirty hours writing this week, there have been times when I was fed up with it. When I didn't want to write anymore. I am growing to like it though. I feel like I am getting better and I can produce better quality in less time. The sheer amount of output does not allow me to be overly critical nor to get feedback on everything (something I have been relying on heavly whenever I was writing something before). Both pieces are definitely not as good as I could make them and if I had more time I would find many ways to make the arguments better.

## Essays über Geopolitik und ein kanadisches Gesetz

Ich glaube nicht, dass ich jemals so viel geschrieben habe wie in dieser Woche. Das Semester neigt sich dem Ende zu und in jedem Kurs stehen die finalen Abgaben in den nächsten ein bis zwei Wochen an. Zusätzlich arbeite ich an meiner Masterbewerbung, worüber ich im Blog nächste Woche mehr erzählen werde. Ich dachte, es könnte interessant sein zu zeigen, was ich diese Woche alles produziert habe.

Der erste Essay war für den Kurs „The Real World of Politics: An Introduction“. Es ist ein Erstsemesterkurs und insgesamt bin ich mir nicht sicher, wie viel ich daraus wirklich mitnehme. Er bleibt sehr oberflächlich und behandelt jede Woche ein komplett neues Thema: Krieg, Demokratie, Liberalismus. Am meisten gelernt habe ich diese Woche über Kanadas koloniale Vergangenheit und darüber, wie sehr dieses Thema heute noch präsent ist. Davon wusste ich vor meiner Ankunft überhaupt nichts. Zum ersten Mal bin ich durch ein sogenanntes „Land Acknowledgment“ darauf gestoßen. Hier ist das der Universität Toronto:

„I (we) wish to acknowledge this land on which the University of Toronto operates. For thousands of years it has been the traditional land of the Huron-Wendat, the Seneca, and the Mississaugas of the Credit. Today, this meeting place is still the home to many Indigenous people from across Turtle Island and we are grateful to have the opportunity to work on this land.”

Im Verlauf des Semesters hatten wir zwei In-Class-Assignments, in denen wir jeweils eine Einleitung und einen argumentierenden Absatz schreiben mussten. Die Abschlussaufgabe war nun ein vollständiger Essay. Wir konnten zwischen drei Themen wählen und etwa 900 Wörter schreiben. Das Thema, das ich ausgewählt habe, lautete:

„This month, the 30th annual United Nations Climate Change Conference (COP30) is taking place in Brazil. The COP meetings have succeeded in decreasing emissions in many industrialized states, but those reductions are not enough to keep global warming under 1.5C. This year, the promise of COP is undermined by the absence of many global leaders, including US President Donald Trump. If the second largest emitter of GHGs rejects climate science and decarbonization, other countries have less incentive to reduce their emissions. Drawing from class materials, the reference article, and external sources, explain why this logic is not necessarily true, and how decarbonization can advance, even without cooperation among states."

Dieser Essay war allerdings kurz im Vergleich zu der 2500-Wörter-Arbeit für „Energy Policy and Environment“. Der Kurs gibt einen breiten Überblick darüber, wie Energiepolitik funktioniert, was berücksichtigt werden muss und wie sich das in die aktuelle Situation in Kanada einordnet. Der Dozent ist Policy Analyst bei Greenpeace und unglaublich freundlich. Nach jeder Vorlesung, die erst um 20 Uhr endet, bietet er eine Diskussionsrunde für Interessierte an. Es ist immer super spannend, jemanden zuzuhören, der seit Jahrzehnten in diesem Bereich arbeitet, und zu erfahren, wie er Einfluss zu nehmen versucht. Nach zwei kürzeren Essays – einer über Hoffnung und einer über einen Policy-Simulator – ging es in der Abschlussarbeit um ein neues kanadisches Gesetz: Bill C-5.

„For the major paper, you will assess how the federal government’s 2025 Building Canada Act (the second part of Bill C-5) will affect Canada’s energy transition. You should include consideration of:

- How does the legislation relate to the institution of federalism?
- How have various interests (e.g. provincial governments, businesses, environmentalists and Indigenous communities) responded to it and why have they taken these positions?

- How will this legislation assist or impede the energy transition?

Please note that you must correctly cite relevant material from the course’s required readings in this assignment. The essay should be no more than 2500 words.“

Nachdem ich diese Woche über dreißig Stunden geschrieben habe, gab es Momente, in denen ich genug hatte. Momente, in denen ich einfach nicht mehr schreiben wollte. Aber langsam beginnt es mir wirklich Spaß zu machen. Ich habe das Gefühl, besser zu werden – sowohl inhaltlich als auch darin, effizienter zu schreiben. Die schiere Menge an Output erlaubt es mir nicht, übermäßig kritisch zu sein oder überall Feedback einzuholen (was ich früher immer gemacht habe). Beide Arbeiten sind definitiv nicht perfekt, und mit mehr Zeit würde ich einiges anders formulieren oder stärker argumentieren.
